This is the 3rd travel industry project we are sharing, and it focuses on a highly niche-specific variation of the travel market.

The company’s main obstacle is competing against highly authoritative provider brands, while large aggregators leverage forum and UGC content with limited visual semantics.



In this type of market, the website still has to rank for commercial terms.

Thus, we heavily focused on the informational segment of the topical map and used it to support the commercial sections through contextual internal linking.



During the project, no backlink-related progress was implemented.



According to Microsoft Bing data, the website received over 500,000 impressions from AI search features, along with 28,000 citations.



Let’s use “safari” as a replacement example for the actual industry.



If the central entity is “safari,” you need to cover all possible attributes and relational contexts around it, such as:



Safari + vaccines

Safari + luggage

Safari + lodge types

Safari + routes

Safari + seasons

Safari + safety

Safari + itineraries

Safari + local regions

Safari + travel requirements



All possible predicates and relations around the safari context should be organized as a connected content network.



Commercial internal links should point to the review pages, main provider pages, itineraries, routes, and engagement-focused sections.



But commercial pages also need the right visual elements, comparison functions, trust signals, and page-level interaction patterns.



A commercial page can rank better temporarily, but it won’t always sustain long-term rankings if the necessary visual and functional elements are missing.

The actual subject of the project is not safari. Safari is only used here as a replacement to explain the methodology.

Good news for SEOs: Google is bringing Generative AI Performance reporting to Google Search Console.

At first, it will mainly help us track AI appearances and impressions, but this is already a meaningful step forward.

Why?

Because we can finally start separating traditional web-result impressions from AI-result impressions more clearly.

For Holistic SEOs, this opens many new analytical layers:

Compare crawl stats and specific user agents with AI appearances.

Compare sentence structures, content freshness, and source responsiveness against AI visibility.

Compare AI performance with the average position of commercial queries.

Compare free-tool query segments with AI appearances to understand whether helpful utility-based assets support broader brand and topical relevance.

For now, the report may not feel fully satisfying because Google is mostly giving impression-level data.

But even with this limitation, there are many new metrics, patterns, and signals that a Holistic SEO can create from it.

AI visibility is not only about being mentioned.

It is about understanding why a source is selected, when it is selected, and which semantic, technical, and behavioral signals support that selection.
